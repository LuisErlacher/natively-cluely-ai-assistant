Likely Cause:
In a real-time Kafka pipeline, the hardest issues usually show up as either consumer lag that keeps climbing, messages being silently dropped, or duplicate processing after a restart. Most of the time the root cause is one of three things: rebalance churn because of a slow or stuck consumer, a misconfigured offset commit, or a partition key that creates hot partitions.

How I Would Investigate:
I would start by checking the consumer group offsets and lag through Kafka's command line tools, then look at the broker logs for rebalance events or under-replicated partitions. If lag is climbing, I'd inspect JVM metrics like GC pauses and check whether any single partition is doing most of the work. I'd also confirm that the offset commit mode matches the semantics we actually need, since at-least-once and exactly-once behave very differently during failure.

Fix:
Depending on what I find, I'd add or tune partition assignments to spread load, increase consumer parallelism for hot keys, and switch to manual offset commits with idempotent processing if duplicates are the problem. For rebalance storms I'd raise the session timeout and make sure the processing loop never blocks longer than the poll interval.

Validation:
I would replay a controlled slice of traffic from an earlier offset and confirm the pipeline produces the same downstream counts without duplicates. Then I'd watch lag and rebalance metrics under sustained load to make sure the fix actually holds.

Prevention:
Going forward I'd add dashboards for consumer lag, rebalance frequency, and processing time per partition, and set alerts before lag crosses a real threshold. Idempotent sinks and a dead-letter topic for poison messages would make the next incident much easier to recover from.
